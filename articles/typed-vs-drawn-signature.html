How-to

Typed vs drawn signatures

Both approaches produce a legitimate signature image—pick the one that matches your audience, brand, and how steady your hand is today.

Typed signatures (script fonts)

Best for: polished letters, proposals, and branded templates where you want consistent curves every time. Typed signatures usually read cleanly at small sizes and pair well with formal typography.

Trade-offs: They can look similar to other people using the same font if they do not customize color, spacing, or supporting initials. For highly personal correspondence, some recipients expect more variation between letters.

Drawn signatures (freehand)

Best for: a natural “wet ink” feel and uniqueness. Drawing can capture pressure-like variation if you use stroke-based tools and avoid shaky ultra-thin lines.

Trade-offs: Harder to reproduce pixel-perfect across years of documents—though that can also make the mark feel authentically yours. On touchpads, practice a few strokes before exporting.

Practical decision framework

  1. Brand documents: start typed; match signature color to logo ink.
  2. Informal agreements: drawn often feels more human.
  3. Forms that require printing: choose thicker strokes and test print at actual size.

Accessibility and clarity

Regardless of mode, prefer dark ink on light paper for scanned workflows. If your PDF will be faxed or photocopied, avoid ultra-light gray signatures that disappear under compression.
